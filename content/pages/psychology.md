
# Space Psychology: The Human Mind Beyond Earth

When living for a long period of time in a small environnement with few other people, it isn't always easy to stay calm and to keep a positive attitude. 

## Psychological Challenges in Space

The profound separation from Earth creates unique psychological stressors. The distance from Earth as well as the communication delays are obviously factors of stress. But added to the inability to leave the confined ship or habitat, and the constant proximity to the others, the crew has to be able to resolve each little problem before it becomes real tensions between them. When selecting the memebers of the missions, the agencies are specifically looking for profiles mature enough to stay calm and discuss when facing a disagreement. Adding on, the international aspect of the agencies and therefore the different origins of the crew memebers might need some time to adjust when discovering cultural differences.

But to compose the perfect team, the individuals most suited for the missions have to be able to function under extreme pressure, to be able to make quick decision and manage crisis as well as being able to adapt.

## Stress
After having spent days, weeks or maybe even years in these missions, the crew is subject to intense and constant stress. There is a non invasive way of mesuring this level of stress. By cutting hair each months, the team is able to follow the level of cortisol, and then deducing the causes.

## Group Activities

To ensure a group dynamic and a deep cohesion, the team is advise to spend ritual moment together such as meals or "apéro" when they can relax and spend time together as friends instead of colleagues. Moreover They often have special activities for birthdays or holidays when each can share its culture with the group.

## Third Quarter Phenomenon

In his book Spacefaring: The Human Dimension, Albert Harrison suggests that there are three stages that emerge over time during long-term missions, whether in space or under (or on) the sea. The first stage is characterized by excitement and anxiety, the second by boredom and depression, and the third by increased aggressiveness and emotional outbursts—something Harrison refers to as “third quarter phenomenon” since it seems to occur just past the mid-point in a mission. What is interesting is that these three stages seem to occur regardless of the total length of a given mission or rotation, be it three weeks, three months, or a year. Understanding and supporting human psychology in space represents a crucial component of successful space exploration and colonization, ensuring that as we expand across the cosmos, we maintain our mental health and humanity.
